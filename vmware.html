Layer 01 · Company & Market Context

Market Context

VMware operates at the foundation of modern enterprise IT. The server virtualization market it dominates is worth ~$8B annually, but the broader cloud infrastructure market it competes in exceeds $200B. Broadcom's acquisition thesis: own the irreplaceable layer between hardware and workloads in large enterprises.

Market Position
~70%+ share in enterprise server virtualization
Dominant hypervisor vendor with no true feature-parity replacement after 25 years of ecosystem development.
Strategic Shift Post-Acquisition
From broad commercial platform to Global 2000-only
Broadcom explicitly de-prioritized SMB and mid-market. If you're not a large enterprise, you're being pushed out.
Key Macro Trend
Hybrid cloud driving demand for consistent infrastructure
Enterprises want a single operational model across on-premises and public cloud. VCF is Broadcom's answer.
Acquisition Closed
November 2023 — restructuring began immediately
Portfolio reduced from 168 SKUs to 4 core bundles. Partner ecosystem cut from 4,500+ providers to ~300.
Layer 02 · Product Overview

Product Portfolio

Post-acquisition, Broadcom dramatically simplified VMware's product portfolio — from 168 SKUs down to 4 core bundles. The strategy: eliminate complexity, force upgrades to flagship, maximize revenue per account.

VMware Cloud Foundation (VCF)
Flagship enterprise bundle
Combines vSphere, vSAN, NSX, and Kubernetes into a full private cloud platform. VCF 9.0 launched June 2025. The primary upsell target for all large accounts.
vSphere Foundation (VVF)
Core compute virtualization
The foundational hypervisor. Most widely deployed enterprise hypervisor globally. Entry point for customers not yet ready for full VCF.
NSX
Software-defined networking & security
Network virtualization layer above physical infrastructure. Key VCF component and significant upsell from vSphere-only deployments.
vSAN
Software-defined storage
Pools server-attached storage into shared datastores. Eliminates need for traditional SAN infrastructure. Bundled into VCF.
Omnissa (Spun Off — July 2024)
End-User Computing — Horizon + Workspace ONE
Sold to KKR for ~$4B. Now an independent company — no longer part of VMware/Broadcom. VDI and UEM products operating under a new brand.
Carbon Black (Merged)
Endpoint Security → Symantec
Merged into Broadcom's Symantec security division. No longer a standalone VMware product. Customers redirected to the Symantec portfolio.
Layer 03 · User & Customer Analysis

Who It Serves

VMware has always had a dual-audience challenge: the IT administrators who live in vCenter every day, and the CIOs who sign multi-million dollar ELAs. Post-Broadcom, this tension has reached a breaking point — the admins are furious, the executives are calculating switching costs.

Primary User
VMware Administrators & Infrastructure Engineers
Deep technical users who manage vCenter environments. Historically brand-loyal. Now VMware's loudest critics — their frustration is the biggest long-term risk to renewal rates.
Economic Buyer
CIO / CTO / VP Infrastructure
Signs the ELA. Evaluates VMware on risk reduction, compliance, and total cost. Currently under intense pressure to justify renewal costs to boards and CFOs.
Target Segment (Post-Broadcom)
Global 2000 Enterprises Only
Broadcom has made its strategic intent explicit. Smaller organizations are effectively being priced out and de-supported through partner ecosystem reduction.
Displaced Segment
SMBs, MSPs, and Mid-Market Companies
Cloud Service Providers cut from 4,500+ globally to ~300 Premier and 12 Pinnacle partners. Smaller customers left without supported migration paths or commercial options.
Layer 04 · Problem & Value Proposition

Value Proposition

VMware's original value prop — run more workloads on less hardware with more flexibility — remains valid. What Broadcom has changed is the commercial proposition: from "essential infrastructure at reasonable cost" to "irreplaceable infrastructure at maximum extractable price."

Functional Value
Still best-in-class — no true replacement exists
No competing hypervisor matches vSphere's full feature set, stability record, or tooling ecosystem built over 25 years of enterprise deployment.
Emotional Value
Severely eroded under Broadcom
VMware was a beloved brand among sysadmins. Abrupt licensing changes, partner cuts, and audit threats have destroyed decades of community goodwill.
Economic Value
Increasingly questioned as prices rise 8x–15x
Customers who could justify the old pricing are now running ROI analyses on migration to Nutanix, Azure Stack HCI, or public cloud workload migration.
Broadcom's Counter-Argument
VCF bundles deliver more value than vSphere alone
Broadcom argues that forcing customers onto VCF — which includes networking, storage, and security — provides better total economics. Most customers disagree with the framing.
Layer 06 · Business Model

Business Model

The Broadcom playbook is a masterclass in monetizing irreplaceable enterprise software. Perpetual licenses eliminated. All products moved to subscription. Pricing shifted to per-core with a minimum floor that dramatically inflates bills for large deployments.

Revenue Model
100% Subscription — Perpetual Licenses Eliminated
As of early 2024, no new perpetual licenses. All products on annual or multi-year subscription. Massive increase in recurring revenue predictability for Broadcom's financials.
Pricing Mechanism
Per Physical CPU Core — 32-Core Packs, 16-Core Minimum
Every CPU counted as at least 16 cores. Sold in packs of 32. Large environments with many low-core CPUs face disproportionate cost increases under this model.
Revenue Performance
$30B VCF Total Contract Value Over 3 Years
Broadcom reported 80 million VCF core entitlements committed. Financially, the strategy is working — even as customer satisfaction reaches historic lows.
Price Impact on Customers
8x to 15x increases — up to 1,200% in extreme cases
No perceived increase in product value. A pure extraction play on a captive customer base where switching costs currently exceed switching motivation for most large accounts.

Layer 08 · Strategic Assessment

Scorecard

VMware as a product remains technically strong. VMware as a business relationship is at an all-time low. Broadcom is executing a deliberate value extraction strategy — betting that switching costs exceed customer frustration. So far, the financials prove them right.

Product Strength
Strong
Still no feature-parity replacement. 25 years of tooling, integrations, and certifications built around vSphere.
Business Model
Aggressive
Subscription-only + per-core pricing = 8–15x price increases. Financially effective, reputationally catastrophic.
Customer Trust
Broken
Decades of goodwill erased. VMware admins — historically its biggest advocates — are now its loudest critics.
Competitive Moat
Eroding
Switching costs remain high, but migration pipelines are filling. Every renewal is now a churn-risk conversation.
SMB / Mid-Market
Abandoned
Intentionally. Broadcom only wants Global 2000. Smaller customers and most MSPs have been effectively cut off.
Innovation Trajectory
Watch
VCF 9.0 shows product investment continues. But customer trust issues overshadow technical progress narratives.

Recommendations

Strategic Recommendations

These recommendations are written from the perspective of a PM inside Broadcom's VMware division — tasked with reversing ecosystem erosion while preserving the revenue model.

0–3 Months
Launch a Transparent Pricing Calculator

Customers are shocked by renewal bills because the per-core model is opaque. A public, self-serve pricing calculator that lets customers model exact costs before renewal reduces surprise and begins rebuilding commercial credibility.

↗ Expected to reduce churn conversations by ~20% and improve NPS among existing accounts
3–12 Months
Create a VCF Migration Credit Program

Customers on vSphere-only deployments are the highest churn risk. A structured credit program giving them financial incentives to move to VCF converts at-risk accounts into bundle customers with higher retention rates.

↗ Target: Convert 30% of vSphere-only Global 2000 accounts to VCF within 12 months
12+ Months
Rebuild Partner Ecosystem with a Community Tier

The partner ecosystem cut damaged long-term platform health. Reintroduce a lightweight Community tier for smaller MSPs and VARs — not as a revenue play, but as an ecosystem health signal that enterprise buyers use to assess platform longevity.

↗ Slows competitive migration narrative; strengthens enterprise perception of platform durability
Layer 09 · Original Thinking

Observations from my PM Lens

If I were handed VMware as a PM tomorrow — inside Broadcom — here is where I would focus my first 90 days. These are my original observations, grounded in the evidence above.

① The Cut List — What I Would Deprioritize
Stop Investing in the Legacy vSphere Standard SKU

The vSphere Standard tier serves smaller customers Broadcom has explicitly decided not to prioritize. Continuing to maintain and support it creates operational overhead without strategic return. Resources should be redirected entirely to VCF feature development and the Global 2000 migration experience. Sunsetting Standard with a clear migration path to VVF or VCF is cleaner than slow neglect.

Evidence: Partner ecosystem reduction signals Broadcom's intent. Standard is the lowest-revenue, highest-support-cost tier.
② The Fix List — What I Would Improve
Redesign the Renewal Communication Journey

The current experience is: customers receive a renewal quote and are shocked by the number. There is no proactive education, no simulation tool, no staged negotiation framework. I would redesign the renewal journey to start 6 months before expiry — with usage reports, cost modelling, and a dedicated CSM touchpoint. The goal: make the number feel earned, not imposed. Success metric: reduction in renewal cycle length and decrease in last-minute escalations to executive level.

Evidence: Customer complaint patterns consistently cite "surprise" as the primary frustration — not price alone.
③ The Build List — What I Would Create
Build a VCF ROI Dashboard — In-Product

Customers who are forced onto VCF need to see its value reflected back at them continuously. I would build an in-product ROI dashboard that shows: infrastructure consolidation savings, security incident prevention metrics, and operational hours saved vs. a disaggregated tooling approach. This transforms VCF from "expensive bundle" to "investment with measurable return" — arming the IT admin to defend renewal to the CFO.

Evidence: 80M cores committed means large customers are in — but they need internal justification tools to stay.
④ Pricing & GTM Gut Check
Introduce a Multi-Year Discount Ladder with Commitments

The current model maximizes short-term extraction but incentivizes annual churn evaluation. I would introduce a meaningful multi-year commitment discount — 15% for 2 years, 25% for 3 years — in exchange for locked core counts. This smooths Broadcom's revenue recognition, reduces annual churn risk, and gives customers a face-saving way to accept higher prices by framing the negotiation around commitment rather than capitulation.

Evidence: $30B TCV committed over 3 years shows large customers will commit — they just need a pricing incentive to do so willingly.
